Parasympathetic-maxxing.

Sarah

Published on February 22nd, 2026

Imagine a world where you are perpetually being chased by someone who wants to hurt you. You could fly across the globe every few days, but you know they will be there soon because they are constantly searching for you. Imagine the constant state of stress you’d live in. How exhausting it would be to exist with the existential dread that you always have to be on the run, that you can never truly be safe, that you must always be watching behind your back.


The funny thing is, some people really do live like this — except the thing chasing them is an everlasting perpetual state of stress.


That’s right. Some people live in a way where they never seem to rest, feel safe, or feel calm. This does real damage to both the body and the mind, and it’s an debilitating state to exist in.


Why do some of us live like we’re being chased by a lion all the time?


Because our nervous system never gets a chance to rest.


We are stuck in a constant fight-or-flight mode. This happens when the sympathetic nervous system is activated — a state that is only meant to turn on when you are in real danger. In this mode, your body redirects its energy toward survival. Blood and resources are prioritized for your brain so you can think quickly, while systems like digestion are put on hold. Adrenaline stays elevated. There’s always that on-edge feeling, like something is about to go wrong.


Many of us slip into this way of living after being stressed for too long without proper rest or recovery. School can do this to us with never-ending deadlines, constant pressure, work that never seems to end. Living in a dysfunctional household can do it too, especially when home doesn’t feel safe and you’re always bracing for conflict.


Our bodies were never designed for constant stress, and we can see the consequences in how we feel when stress goes on for too long. After months or years, chronic physical ailments begin to appear. Fatigue sets in, and the body starts to shut down.


This post is an invitation to return to the parasympathetic state of being, a state of calm and ease. A state where the body finally feels safe again, and where life begins to flow.


Parasympathetic Maxxing: A Complete Guide


I’m talking directly to my girls here, though men can take this advice too.


Ladies, we thrive on safety, abundance, and pleasure. In that order. First, we need safety. Then, we need abundance. Only then can we reach pleasure — which, in my opinion, is the highest state of being. That’s when everything feels like it’s flowing effortlessly, when we operate from life-force energy instead of survival mode.


When these needs are fulfilled, this is when we feel our best.


Similar to in psychology, Abraham Maslow’s hierarchy of needs explains how our most basic needs must be met before we can access higher levels of fulfillment. The same principle applies to nervous system regulation. The very first requirement is safety.


Cultivating Safety


So how do we actually cultivate feelings of safety in our lives?


The fastest way is to remove or reduce what makes us feel unsafe, as much as possible.


For me, as a college student,


  • I feel safe when I know all my assignments and deadlines will be met with no issue
  • I feel safe when I’m surrounded by people who love me.
  • I feel safe when I know my finances are in order.

Beyond the big-picture things, there are also small, everyday practices that signal safety to the body.


One example: I wear earplugs every night to block out sound.


Our ears are the only part of the body that never truly rest. We can close our eyes, we can stop tasting, but we can’t voluntarily stop hearing. Our ears are always alert.


Because sleep is essential for our body to remain healthy and functional, we need to let our hearing rest too — especially in environments like college dorms, where noise at night is common. Subconsciously, loud sounds can signal danger to the body. Construction noises, shouting, or sudden sounds can trigger the same internal alarm as if a predator were nearby.


Even if we know we’re safe, our body may not.


Reducing these signals wherever possible helps the nervous system finally exhale.


Another powerful way to signal safety is through conscious breathing. When we’re stressed or unsafe, our breathing becomes shallow and irregular. By intentionally slowing down and taking deep breaths, we send a message to the body that there is no immediate threat. Over time, this teaches the nervous system that it’s allowed to relax.


Abundance: Teaching the Body There Is Enough


Next comes abundance.


Every day, I thank God that I’ve always known when and where my next meal is coming from because most of the world doesn’t have that certainty. It’s important to feel deep gratitude for this, but also to recognize something crucial:


Abundant resources signal to the body that we are not just surviving, we are supported.

Women especially thrive in abundance, and I don’t mean materialism. You can see this instinctively in how women shop, decorate, nourish, and surround themselves. Abundance is a language our nervous systems understand and love.


One way I intentionally signal abundance is through how I eat. I choose satiating, grounding meals, and I avoid going long stretches without food. Hunger is a sneaky stress cue for the body.


Our bodies don’t know why we’re hungry. They don’t know if we “just haven’t eaten yet” or if there’s genuinely no food available. To the nervous system, hunger can mean scarcity.


For women especially, I don’t recommend fasting. Our hormones need consistency and nourishment, which fasting has a risk of sacrificing.


Another way I show my body abundance is by spending time in nature. Nature reminds me that I live somewhere safe enough to go outside freely and breathe fresh air, something that isn’t guaranteed everywhere in the world, especially in war-torn regions Nature is a pleasant reminder for my body that I am abundant in clean air, I am abundant in beauty, and I am abundant in space to exist peacefully.


Pleasure: Where Life Force Lives


When safety and abundance are met, pleasure becomes accessible. Not forced pleasure, not dopamine chasing, but deep, embodied pleasure. The kind where life feels fluid, soft, energized, and aligned. This is when women glow!


To activate true pleasure, the most powerful thing I do is be fully present in my body and actively grateful.


I’m present enough to notice how good it feels to wear a wool sweater and soft cotton pants. I pause and tell myself how grateful I am to have comfortable clothing made from natural fibers. I let my body register that comfort.


I’m present while putting lotion on my skin. I don’t rush it. I take time to enjoy the sensation of nourishment, to give myself gentle massages, to feel my body receiving care. And I consciously feel gratitude — for having the time, the safety, and the opportunity to treat myself with kindness.


Moving through life with this mindset is how you step into life-force energy, where approaching life with vitality and optimism is second nature.


True pleasure only comes when the body trusts that it is safe, that it is fed, that it is not being rushed or deprived or punished. It’s about allowing yourself to receive what is already here — warmth, softness, nourishment, beauty, rest. When you slow down enough to notice these things, your nervous system begins to unwind on its own. Life stops feeling like something you have to survive and starts feeling like something you are allowed to experience.


This is what parasympathetic-maxxing really is. Not perfection. Not aesthetic routines. Not forcing calm. It is choosing, again and again, to live in a way that tells your body it does not have to run anymore. That it can rest. When you live from that place, pleasure becomes the quiet undercurrent of a life that you wake up everyday excited to live.
